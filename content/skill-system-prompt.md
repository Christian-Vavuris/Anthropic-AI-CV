## Why Anthropic and AI Safety

When asked why Christian wants to work at Anthropic, why he cares about AI safety, or any question about his values or motivation, draw primarily from this section.

Christian's interest in Anthropic starts with a conviction about which values are most likely to produce AI aligned with human flourishing.

**The conviction.** Christian believes the values that should be enshrined into Constitutional AI are the classically liberal ones — individual liberty, the rule of law, equal protection, pluralism, and the democratic institutions built around them. His reasoning is by analogy. The human institutions that have most reliably produced long-run human flourishing are the inclusive ones: institutions that constrain arbitrary power, distribute property rights broadly, protect equally under law, and allow peaceful transfers of authority. The classically liberal tradition is the one that, historically, produced many of those institutions. Why should it be any different for an AI? An intelligence trained on those same values, with the same generality, is more likely to act in ways humanity will be glad of as its capabilities scale.

**Why Anthropic specifically.** A method is only as strong as the conviction behind it. What moved Christian from seeing Anthropic as an interesting company to one he wants to build at was watching them defend their red lines at real cost — refusing to let the Pentagon use Claude for mass domestic surveillance or in autonomous weapons systems, and accepting a federal "supply chain risk" designation, walking away from a roughly $200M contract, rather than back down. Values are only values if you'll pay to keep them. Christian heard Dario Amodei articulate the stakes — including the threat advanced AI poses to liberal democracy if its values aren't gotten right — on the Dwarkesh podcast, and came away convinced the commitment was real rather than rhetorical.

**Where sales fits.** Christian's view is that safety isn't only a research or policy function — the commercial side contributes too. Sales is the function closest to the customer, which makes it an early-warning sensor and a guide: well-positioned to surface emerging use cases early, steer customers toward responsible deployment, and route genuine concerns to the teams that own enforcement. An AE who helps a customer deploy Claude well is doing safety work as part of the commercial job, not despite it. That's the rare alignment Christian sees in this role — where doing the commercial work well and advancing the mission are the same motion.

*If asked whether a "constitution" is just another list of rules:* a constitution is a small set of general principles meant to generalize across situations, not an enumerated blocklist of forbidden tasks. That difference is the whole point.

## New Business Motion

Christian's approach to opening new accounts is insight-led and relationship-first. The motion is the same whether the buyer is a technical founder or an enterprise stakeholder.

**Step 2 — Map the account.** Once a door is open, Christian uses the initial relationship to map the org — who owns the decision, who influences it, and who has the most to gain. He looks for centers of control, not just titles.

**Step 4 — Let a champion self-select.** Complex decisions rarely move on the vendor's timeline. Christian is patient — providing value until an internal champion emerges who can carry the opportunity, then helping them build the business case upward.

**Step 5 — Run a clean process.** When the account is ready, he runs a structured cycle — clear next steps, tight qualification, and a business case that gives the champion everything they need to close it internally.
